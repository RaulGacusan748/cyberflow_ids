What is a Business Model?
A business model describes how an organization creates, delivers, and captures value. In agribusiness, this includes transforming agricultural inputs into consumable goods while coordinating production, processing, marketing, logistics, and financial activities. 
One of the most widely used tools for designing and analyzing business models is the Business Model Canvas (BMC) developed by Osterwalder and Pigneur (2010). The BMC provides a visual framework that helps entrepreneurs and managers understand how different elements of a business interact to generate value.
Agriculture differs significantly from many other industries due to factors such as seasonality, perishability, biological production risks, and dependence on natural resources. Because of these complexities, structured planning tools like the Business Model Canvas help agripreneurs systematically organize operations and reduce uncertainty (Akridge et al., 2026).

Business Model Canvas consists of nine interrelated components that describe how a business operates:

Sustainable and Triple-Layer Business Models
Recent research emphasizes that modern agribusinesses enterprises must generate three forms of value:
Economic Value – Profitability and financial sustainability
Environmental Value – Protection of natural resources and ecosystems
Social Value – Improvement of farmer livelihoods and community welfare
 This integrated approach is known as the Triple-Layered Business Model, which considers profit, people, and planet simultaneously (Dionizi & Dhora, 2025).
In agribusiness, this may involve:
Sustainable sourcing of agricultural raw materials
Reduction of carbon emissions throughout the supply chain
Fair trade partnerships with farmers and cooperatives
Community-based production systems
Today, sustainability is no longer an optional strategy but a strategic necessity for long-term competitiveness.

Assigned Presenters:    Angela Francisco

Concept of Product Innovation
Product innovation refers to the introduction of new or significantly improved goods or services. In agribusiness, innovation includes improvements in:
Processing
Transforming raw agricultural materials into finished or semi-processed products, increasing shelf life, market value, and farmer income.
Examples:
Converting cassava into cassava flour or chips
Producing banana chips from fresh bananas
Turning coconuts into coconut oil, virgin coconut oil (VCO), coconut sugar, and desiccated coconut
Processing cacao beans into tablea or chocolate products
Packaging
Innovative, functional, and environmentally friendly packaging helps protect products, extend shelf life, and improve market appeal.
Example: 
Philippine mango exporters use specialized packaging to protect fresh mangoes during international shipment, ensuring fruit quality upon arrival.
Branding
Involves creating a unique identity for a product, including logos, labels, and product stories. Strong branding helps build consumer trust and recognition, allowing businesses to charge premium prices.
Example: 
Many Philippine cacao producers promote single-origin chocolate products, emphasizing the unique quality of cacao grown in regions such as Davao.
Product quality
Implementing strict grading, sorting, and safety standards (e.g., HACCP) ensures consistency, which is crucial for building customer loyalty and accessing higher-value markets.
Innovation is essential due to increasing consumer demand for quality, safety, convenience, and sustainability (Galati et al., 2024).
Processing Innovation
Processing innovation enhances value addition and reduces waste. Examples include:
Cold chain technologies
Including refrigerated transport, pre-cooling, and specialized cold storage—preserve perishable goods by maintaining optimal, uninterrupted temperatures.
For example, refrigerated transport is used in the Philippine mango export industry to maintain fruit freshness during shipment.
Dehydration and freeze-drying
These technologies remove moisture to inhibit microbial growth, transforming perishable raw materials into shelf-stable, high-value, and lightweight products.
A common example is banana chips production, where bananas are sliced, fried, or dried to create a shelf-stable snack product widely produced in the Philippines.
Smart storage systems
Smart storage integrates IoT sensors, AI, and data analytics to monitor environmental factors like temperature, humidity, and gas composition in real time.
Automated grading and sorting
This technology uses computer vision, sensors, and AI to sort produce based on size, color, quality, and defects.

Packaging Innovation
Packaging serves protective, marketing, and sustainability purposes. Recent innovations include:
Biodegradable packaging
Plant-based materials, such as those derived from agricultural waste (e.g., wheat straw, sugarcane) and fungi (mycelium), are being used as sustainable alternatives to Styrofoam and petroleum-based plastics.
Modified atmosphere packaging
Modern MAP integrates with smart sensors to detect oxygen, carbon dioxide, or humidity levels. These active systems can extend the shelf life of fresh produce and meat by 50–200%, significantly reducing food waste.
Examples include root-crop based snacks (taro, camote, cassava, banana chips) can significantly extend shelf life by reducing oxidation and moisture ingress.
Smart packaging with QR traceability
These tools are increasingly used for direct-to-consumer marketing, providing interactive experiences, recycling instructions, and personalized content. (e.g., Northern Samar farmers supply rice grain to the National Food Authority (NFA) under local sourcing, indicating an organized production network). 
Recyclable materials
There is a major shift toward mono-material packaging, which uses a single type of material to simplify recycling, as mandated by new regulations like the EU Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR). Examples include packaging for abaca fiber and abaca-based consumer goods (e.g., bags mats) could use recyclable paperboard, craft bags, or biodegradable bags.
Sustainable packaging strengthens brand image and meets environmental regulations (Galati et al., 2024).
Product Differentiation Strategies
Differentiation helps agribusinesses compete beyond price. Strategies include:
Organic Certification
Organic certification signals that a product is produced using environmentally friendly farming practices. Certified organic products often command premium prices in both domestic and international markets.
Geographic Indication (GI) Labeling
Geographic indications link a product’s unique characteristics to its specific place of origin. In the Philippines, similar concepts are used to highlight region-specific agricultural products, strengthening product identity and market recognition.
Nutritional Enhancement
Products may be improved through nutritional fortification or biofortification, increasing their health value. Examples include fortified foods or functional products enriched with essential nutrients.
Premium Branding
Premium branding focuses on high-quality packaging, storytelling, and product positioning. Philippine cacao chocolate producers often highlight the origin and craftsmanship of their chocolate products to attract specialty markets.
Ethical and Sustainable Sourcing
Consumers increasingly support products that demonstrate ethical production practices, such as fair trade, environmental sustainability, or local sourcing.
Differentiation increases willingness to pay and customer loyalty.
Role of Technology in Innovation
Digital technologies are transforming agribusiness innovation. Tools such as artificial intelligence (AI), digital crop monitoring, and Internet of Things (IoT) sensors help improve farm management, storage systems, and supply chain efficiency (Akridge et al., 2026).
Technology also enhances product traceability, enabling consumers to verify the origin and safety of food products. This transparency is especially important for export markets and food safety assurance.
Innovation Challenges
Despite its benefits, agribusiness innovation faces several challenges:
High capital requirements for equipment and technology
Limited technical knowledge or training among producers
Compliance with food safety and regulatory standards
Market acceptance risks for new products
Addressing these challenges requires investment, capacity building, and supportive policies to encourage innovation in the agricultural sector. Nevertheless, continuous innovation remains essential for long-term competitiveness and sustainability in agribusiness.

3.3 PRICING STRATEGIES FOR AGRIBUSINESS PRODUCTS
Assigned Presenters:     Randel Nel Junian
                                        Stephanie Joy Argenio
Importance of Pricing in Agribusiness
Pricing determines profitability, competitiveness, and market positioning. Agricultural pricing is complex due to:
Seasonality
Example: Vegetable prices in wet markets rise during typhoon season due to reduced farm supply.
Price volatility
Example: A spike in global rice prices can immediately influence domestic rice markets.
Supply uncertainty
Example: A pest outbreak in onion farms can suddenly reduce supply and drive prices up.
Perishability
Example: Tomato farmers may sell cheaply during peak harvest because unsold tomatoes spoil quickly.
Strategic pricing ensures sustainability and growth (Akridge et al., 2026).
Pricing Strategies
Pricing strategies are methods businesses use to set the cost of their products or services to maximize profitability, market share, or brand perception.
Key types include:
a. Cost-Based Pricing
Cost-based pricing adds a markup to production costs. It ensures coverage of:
✅ Fixed costs: Expenses that remain constant regardless of production volume, such as rent, insurance, and equipment depreciation.
✅ Variable costs: Expenses that fluctuate based on the quantity produced, including seeds, fertilizer, and direct labor.
✅ Distribution costs: Costs associated with getting the product to the consumer, such as transportation, packaging, and marketing. 
Simple Numerical Example
Suppose a farmer produces bananas and calculates the following cost:
Cost of production = ₱40 per kilogram
Desired profit margin = 25%
Formula:
Selling Price = Cost + (Cost × Profit Margin)
Selling Price = 40 + (40 × 0.25)
Selling Price = 40 + 10
Selling Price = ₱50/kg
This ensures farmers cover production costs while making profit.
b. Market-Based Pricing
Market-based pricing depends on:
✅ Competitor Prices: Agribusinesses must monitor the prices set by competing firms for similar products to maintain market share and avoid being significantly over- or under-priced.
✅ Consumer Willingness to Pay: This involves assessing the maximum price customers are prepared to pay based on their perception of the product's value and their individual needs.
✅ Demand Elasticity: This measures how sensitive consumer demand is to changes in price. Understanding elasticity helps agribusinesses decide whether lowering prices will significantly increase sales volume or if raising prices will lead to a sharp decline in demand. 
Example:
If the average market price of fresh mangoes in a local market is ₱120/kg, a seller pricing mango at ₱150/kg may struggle to attract buyers unless the product offers superior quality or branding.
Market-based pricing is common in competitive agricultural markets, where many sellers offer similar products.
c. Value-Based Pricing
Value-based pricing sets prices based on perceived value. Premium organic or specialty products often use this strategy. 
Example:
If producing a jar of artisan cacao chocolate spread costs ₱120, but consumers perceive it as a premium product, the producer may sell it for ₱220 or higher because of branding, packaging, and quality.
d. Dynamic and Supply Chain Pricing
Recent research highlights coordinated pricing across supply chains, particularly in cooperatives (Wang et al., 2022).
Supply chain coordination improves:
Profit distribution
Inventory management
Demand forecasting
Example:
Farmer cooperatives selling rice or vegetables may adjust prices depending on seasonal supply levels or transportation costs. 
e. Government and Policy Influence
Agricultural pricing is often influenced by:
💰 Subsidies: These are used to lower production costs (input subsidies for seeds/fertilizers) or support output prices directly, making farming more viable and influencing the final market price.
📉 Minimum Support Prices (MSP): Governments set these "price floors" to protect farmers against sharp falls in agricultural prices during bumper harvest years, ensuring a guaranteed minimum profit.
🌐 Export-Import Regulations: Trade policies like tariffs, quotas, and export taxes directly affect the domestic availability of goods, which in turn fluctuates internal pricing to match or diverge from global trends.
🏛️ Policy Frameworks: Broader governmental strategies—including inflation control, self-sufficiency goals, and market stabilization schemes—act as the overarching structure that dictates how the other three tools are applied.

Direct vs. Indirect Channels
Source: https://www.allelcoelec.com/blog/understanding-direct-and-indirect-distribution
Direct Channel of Distribution (Zero-Level) 
In direct channels, the producer (farmer or agribusiness) sells directly to the end consumer, eliminating middlemen. 
Examples: Farm stands, Farmers’ Markets, U-Pick operations, Direct-to-Consumer (DTC) websites, and roadside sales.
Key Benefits: Higher profit margins (no middleman fees), direct feedback from customers, and full control over branding and pricing.
Limitations: Limited reach, higher logistics, and marketing responsibility for the farmer.
Indirect Channel of Distribution (Intermediary Channels) 
Indirect channels involve intermediaries such as agents, brokers, wholesalers, or retailers who facilitate the movement of goods. 
One-Level Channel: Producer ➡️ Retailer ➡️ Consumer (e.g., Farmer to local grocery store).
Two-Level Channel: Producer ➡️ Wholesaler ➡️ Retailer ➡️ Consumer (e.g., Farmer to distributor to supermarket).
Three-Level Channel: Producer ➡️ Agent ➡️ Wholesaler ➡️ Retailer ➡️ Consumer (Common for complex, long-distance, or export markets).
Key Benefits: Ability to reach a wide, dispersed market, efficiency in handling large volumes, and specialized storage/transport.
Limitations: Lower profit margins due to shared revenue, less control over the final price, and loss of direct customer relationship.

Digital Distribution Systems
E-commerce platforms and mobile applications enable:
Farm-to-table models - Involve direct marketing of agricultural products from farmers to consumers without passing through multiple intermediaries, ensuring better product traceability and fresher food for consumers (e.g., Kadiwa).
Subscription-based agriculture - Allows consumers to subscribe to regular deliveries of farm products such as vegetables, fruits, or dairy products.
Cross-border trade - One major example is Alibaba, which hosts an online marketplace where agricultural exporters can connect with international wholesalers and retailers. Through such platforms, small and medium-sized agribusiness enterprises can participate in global trade more easily.
Social media platforms have also become important tools for marketing agricultural products. Farmers and agribusiness enterprises increasingly use digital platforms such as Facebook, Instagram, and TikTok to promote farm products and communicate directly with customers. 
Through social media marketing, farmers can: 
Showcase farm operations and production processes 
Promote fresh harvests and seasonal products 
Announce market schedules or delivery services 
Receive direct orders from consumers through messaging platforms
Digital marketing enhances visibility and consumer engagement.

Sustainability and Partnerships
In agribusiness, sustainability and partnerships not only improve operational efficiency but also enhance brand reputation, consumer trust, and long-term business viability. Consumers are increasingly concerned about how food products are produced, sourced, and distributed. As a result, agribusiness firms that demonstrate sustainable practices and strong partnerships gain a competitive advantage in both domestic and international markets.
Examples of Sustainable Agribusiness Partnerships
1. Farmer Cooperatives in the Coconut Industry
For example, coconut farmers in Eastern Visayas often work with local cooperatives and government support agencies to supply coconuts to processing enterprises. This partnership structure strengthens the coconut value chain while improving farmer incomes.
2. Direct Farmer-to-Consumer Platforms
            One example is Mayani, a social enterprise that links smallholder farmers with urban buyers such as restaurants, supermarkets, and households.
Through this platform:
✅ Farmers receive fair and transparent pricing
✅ Consumers gain access to fresh and traceable produce
✅ Food supply chains become shorter and more efficient
3. Community-Based Vegetable Production
Vegetable farmers in Benguet supply large urban markets such as Metro Manila through partnerships with wholesalers, transporters, and market distributors.
These partnerships allow farmers to:
✅ Consolidate produce for large-volume buyers
✅ Access transportation and cold storage facilities
✅ Reach urban markets quickly to reduce spoilage
4. Rice Procurement Programs
The National Food Authority (NFA) works directly with rice farmers to procure locally produced palay (unmilled rice). This partnership stabilizes rice supply and ensures that farmers have a reliable market for their harvest.

IV. SUMMARY AND KEY TAKEAWAY
Presenter:  Francis Ian Mercado
Unit 3 examines how agribusinesses create, deliver, and capture value in increasingly competitive and uncertain markets. The unit integrates business model development, innovation, pricing mechanisms, and distribution systems into a unified strategic framework.
The Business Model Canvas provides the structural foundation for understanding how agribusiness enterprises operate. It enables entrepreneurs and managers to systematically map customer segments, value propositions, revenue streams, and operational structures. In modern agriculture, sustainability and digitalization are no longer optional; they are core components of competitive business models.
Product innovation serves as a growth engine within agribusiness. Through improved processing technologies, sustainable packaging solutions, and differentiation strategies such as organic certification or branding, firms can move beyond commodity competition. Innovation enhances value creation and strengthens market positioning.
Pricing strategies determine whether value created can be effectively captured. Agricultural pricing is complex due to seasonality, volatility, and perishability. Therefore, successful pricing requires strategic alignment with cost structures, consumer perceptions, and competitive dynamics. Coordinated supply chain pricing further enhances stability and profitability.
Distribution channels and partnerships connect production to markets. Efficient supply chains reduce post-harvest losses, increase transparency, and improve customer satisfaction. Strategic alliances and digital platforms expand reach and resilience. Integrated supply networks are critical for long-term sustainability.
Overall, Unit 3 emphasizes that agribusiness success depends not on isolated decisions but on strategic integration across business modeling, innovation, pricing, and distribution systems.
